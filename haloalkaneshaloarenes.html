Haloalkanes & Haloarenes

Introduction

Haloalkanes and haloarenes are organic compounds containing halogens (F, Cl, Br, I). Haloalkanes have halogens attached to aliphatic carbons, while haloarenes have halogens attached to aromatic rings.

Classification

Haloalkanes

Classified based on carbon attached to halogen:

  • Primary (1°): Attached to 1 other carbon (CH₃CH₂Cl)
  • Secondary (2°): Attached to 2 carbons (CH₃CHClCH₃)
  • Tertiary (3°): Attached to 3 carbons ((CH₃)₃CCl)

Haloarenes

Halogen attached directly to an aromatic ring (C₆H₅Cl). Reactivity is lower due to resonance.

Nomenclature

IUPAC: Name parent chain, number carbon, add halogen prefix. Example: CH₃CH₂Br → Bromoethane

Common: Alkyl group + Halide. Example: CH₃CH₂Cl → Ethyl chloride

Preparation of Haloalkanes

From Alkanes

Free radical halogenation: CH₄ + Cl₂ → CH₃Cl + HCl (UV light)

From Alcohols

Using HX: R–OH + HX → R–X + H₂O

Using SOCl₂: ROH + SOCl₂ → RCl + SO₂ + HCl

From Alkenes

Addition of HX: CH₂=CH₂ + HCl → CH₃CH₂Cl

From Grignard Reagents

RMgX + RX → R–R’ (coupling reaction)

Preparation of Haloarenes

Electrophilic Substitution

Benzene + Cl₂ → C₆H₅Cl (FeCl₃ catalyst)

Sandmeyer Reaction

C₆H₅N₂⁺Cl⁻ + CuCl → C₆H₅Cl + N₂

Physical Properties

Haloalkanes

  • Colorless liquids (mostly)
  • Higher BP than alkanes due to dipole-dipole forces
  • Slightly soluble in water, miscible with organic solvents

Haloarenes

  • Less reactive than haloalkanes
  • Resonance stabilizes C–X bond

Chemical Reactions

Nucleophilic Substitution

SN2 (1° haloalkanes) – CH₃CH₂Br + OH⁻ → CH₃CH₂OH

SN1 (3° haloalkanes) – (CH₃)₃CBr + H₂O → (CH₃)₃COH

Elimination (Dehydrohalogenation)

CH₃CH₂Br + alc. KOH → CH₂=CH₂ + HBr

Wurtz Reaction

2 R–X + 2 Na → R–R + 2 NaX

Electrophilic Substitution (Haloarenes)

C₆H₅Cl + HNO₃ → C₆H₄Cl–NO₂ (H₂SO₄ catalyst)

Uses & Environmental Effects

Uses: Solvents, refrigerants, pesticides, pharmaceuticals, dyes, polymers.

Environmental: CFCs damage ozone; halogenated aromatics may be toxic and persistent.
